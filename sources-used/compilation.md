# Compilation

> What all the sources agree on, stripped of jargon.

This is a cross-source analysis of what AA/12-Step programs, men's groups, and emergency services peer support models share. Despite different contexts and different languages, they converge on the same core.

## The core — what every tradition agrees on

**Confidentiality with limits**
Everything shared stays in the room. Every tradition names this as foundational. Every serious tradition also names the exceptions: imminent risk to self or others overrides confidentiality. Trust is built on honesty, not on promises no one can keep.

**Safety and nonviolence**
Meetings must be physically and emotionally safe. Disruptive or threatening behaviour is not tolerated. This isn't about comfort — it's about the basic conditions that make honest sharing possible.

**Respectful communication**
No ridicule. No bullying. No unsolicited advice. Share from your own experience; don't project it onto others. The difference between support and interference is whether someone asked for it.

**"I" statements**
Speak from your own experience. Generalisations ("men always…", "people like that…") create distance and invite debate. Personal truth creates connection.

**Right to pass**
No one is compelled to share. Readiness can't be forced. Voluntary participation respects where people actually are.

**Balanced airtime**
The group belongs to everyone in it. No one dominates. Good facilitation holds the space without taking it over.

**Punctuality and consistency**
Meetings start and end on time. The container has to hold its shape for the work inside it to mean anything. Groups that can't keep time are signalling — often unconsciously — that nothing here is quite real.

**Personal responsibility**
Members maintain the culture. This isn't the facilitator's job — it belongs to everyone. A group gets the culture it tolerates.

**Clear roles**
Peer support is not therapy. Knowing the difference matters — to the person facilitating, and to the people being supported. When something is beyond what the group can hold, say so and point elsewhere.

**Group conscience**
Disputes and policy questions belong to the whole group, not to any individual. Collective decision-making is itself a practice of equality.

**Accountability**
Groups establish consequences for repeated violations. This isn't punitive — it's honest. A group that tolerates everything that damages it won't survive.

**Present-moment attention**
Phones away. Full presence. The work requires it, and so do the people in the room.

## What the sources don't share

Clinical and formal models (fire/EMS, veterans, APA) carry legal obligations — duty to warn, duty to report, signed confidentiality agreements. Men's circles and peer groups typically don't.

Faith-based models (AA) carry spiritual frameworks that some groups will share and others won't.

This document takes the bones and leaves the rest.
