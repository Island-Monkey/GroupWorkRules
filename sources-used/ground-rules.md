# Ground Rules: AA, 12-Step, Veterans & Emergency Services

> Taking inspiration from faith-based addiction groups and peer support models built under pressure.

These traditions didn't develop their frameworks in seminar rooms. They developed them in church basements, fire stations, and veterans' centres — places where the stakes were real and the people weren't there to theorise.

That's worth something.

## What they share

Across AA, 12-Step programs, military peer support (CIPSRT), and emergency services models, the same principles appear again and again:

- **Anonymity as a cornerstone** — what's said here, stays here. In some traditions this is spiritual; in all of them it's practical.
- **Group conscience** — disputes and decisions belong to the group, not to any individual.
- **Right to pass** — no one is compelled to speak. Readiness can't be forced.
- **Structure as container** — meetings start and end on time. Roles are clear. Consistency builds trust.
- **Escalation protocols** — peer support is not therapy. When something is beyond the group's capacity, it says so and points elsewhere.

## What's different

AA and 12-Step carry a spiritual framework that not every group will share. The veterans' and emergency services models are more formal — signed agreements, documented ethics, defined duty-to-report obligations.

Men's circles tend to be lighter on procedure and heavier on culture. The risk there is that culture without structure can drift.

## What we take from this

The bones. The commitment to confidentiality, the right to pass, the discipline of timekeeping, the honesty about what a peer group can and can't hold.

The spirit of the thing, not the letter.
